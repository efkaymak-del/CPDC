What This Is

EIRINI is a digital civic space where citizens engage with the Cyprus problem on their own terms, on their own schedule. It maps the full landscape of opinion across both communities — not to find artificial consensus, but to make visible what people actually think, feel, and want.

Beyond Tokenism

EIRINI integrates Participatory Action Research (PAR) methodology. This is not a static poll or a top-down consultation. It operates at the level of 'Delegated Power' (Arnstein), embedding citizens at the center of the peace process through iterative Reflection-Action loops.

Agonistic Pluralism

Following Chantal Mouffe's framework, EIRINI does not force a sanitized, rational consensus. It is a safe institutional container that transforms raw antagonism into democratic agonism. Disagreement is not failure; it is constitutive of democratic life.

Who Built This

EIRINI is an initiative of the Cyprus Peace and Dialogue Center (CPDC), developed in parallel with the consultative body process agreed by the leaders in July 2025. It is designed as the digital infrastructure for inclusive civic participation — a mirror that shows negotiators what their communities actually want.

How Each Side Sees It

The same events, framed differently. Neither framing is endorsed by this platform.

Greek Cypriot Perspective

The Republic of Cyprus is the sole internationally recognized state. Any solution must respect the acquis communautaire and EU membership. The BBF framework remains the basis for negotiations. Turkey's two-state rhetoric undermines the process.

Erhürman's election is cautiously welcomed but concrete steps — not rhetoric — are needed. The consultative body must include genuine civil society, not political proxies.

Turkish Cypriot Perspective

Turkish Cypriots are equal co-founders of the Republic, not a minority. Decades of BBF negotiations have produced no result. Any new process must be "without prejudice" to outcome — all options must remain on the table.

Erhürman's presidency represents a mandate for engagement but also for ending isolation and unfair treatment. Political equality and sovereign status are non-negotiable prerequisites.

The Community Mirror

How does each community discuss the Cyprus problem amongst itself? Not what they say to each other — but what they say about each other. The patterns revealed here are often more honest than any joint forum.

Greek Cypriots discussing Turkish Cypriots

Aggregate patterns from intracommunal discourse

Dominant narrative: Turkish Cypriots are seen sympathetically as individuals but political leadership was viewed as an extension of Ankara. Erhürman's election introduced cautious optimism.

Sentiment: 40% skeptical · 30% cautious · 30% hopeful

Recurring concern: 'Even if we agree, Turkey will veto.' This fatalism coexists with genuine desire for contact and normalcy — a tension the political class rarely acknowledges.

Sentiment: 55% pessimistic · 45% ambivalent

AI Pattern Detection

A significant gap exists between private and public discourse. In intracommunal settings, Greek Cypriots express more willingness to accept power-sharing than they would in a bicommunal forum. Social stigma around 'conceding too much' suppresses moderate positions in public.

Turkish Cypriots discussing Greek Cypriots

Aggregate patterns from intracommunal discourse

Dominant narrative: Greek Cypriots are perceived as unwilling to accept Turkish Cypriots as political equals. The 2004 Annan Plan referendum is frequently cited — 'we said yes, they said no, and we were punished for it.'

Sentiment: 50% resentful · 30% resigned · 20% open

Under-reported narrative: A growing segment — particularly younger, urban, secular — expresses frustration not primarily with Greek Cypriots but with Turkey's increasing influence. 'We didn't fight for independence from one side to become dependent on another.'

Sentiment: 45% defiant · 35% anxious · 20% hopeful

AI Pattern Detection

Turkish Cypriot discourse reveals a three-way split: those who prefer two states, those who prefer federation with equality guarantees, and a growing cohort expressing fatigue with the entire framework debate. The third group is the fastest-growing and least represented in negotiations.

Cross-Mirror Insight

Both communities exhibit a 'public-private gap' — positions expressed within the community are more flexible than those expressed across the divide. This suggests that social pressure within each community, not fundamental incompatibility between them, may be the primary obstacle to settlement. A platform that makes this gap visible could fundamentally change the negotiation dynamic.

Stage 1: Bicommunal World Café Active

Greek Cypriot and Turkish Cypriot nodes rotate through 4 policy tables (Security, Territory, Property, Governance). AI translates input in real-time, preventing linguistic dominance. Experts observe but do not steer.

Stage 2: AI Semantic Extraction Waiting

Instead of mapping polarized slogans, the AI extracts underlying shared themes and human needs (e.g., 'Implementation Security', 'Predictable Timelines') to the center. Jargon is demystified; needs are surfaced.

Stage 3: Expert Removal Protocol Waiting

To ensure cognitive equity, Expert/UN nodes are removed from the deliberative space. The AI generates a 'Bridging Proposal' based purely on semantic clusters synthesized from citizen input. No elite capture.

Stage 4: Agonistic Consensus Waiting

Citizens converge toward the bridging proposal. Crucially, GC and TC nodes remain visually clustered in distinct identities. This proves Agonistic Pluralism: agreement without assimilation or identity erasure.

Theoretical Framework: Agonistic Pluralism vs. Deliberative Consensus

EIRINI is grounded in Chantal Mouffe's agonistic pluralism rather than Jürgen Habermas's deliberative democracy model. While Habermasian approaches seek rational consensus through structured dialogue, agonistic approaches recognize that political conflict is constitutive of democratic life.

In the Cyprus context, this distinction is critical: a consensus-seeking platform implicitly privileges the status quo framework (BBF) and marginalizes positions that challenge it — including those held by significant portions of both communities. The Habermasian model presupposes equality that doesn't exist and treats dissensus as failure rather than legitimate political expression.

The platform's design follows three agonistic principles: (1) all positions are legitimate political expressions, not obstacles to be overcome; (2) the goal is transformation of antagonism into agonism, not elimination of conflict; (3) the platform reveals the landscape of opinion rather than steering it toward convergence.

Participatory Action Research (PAR) & the World Café Method

This initiative explicitly rejects tokenistic consultation. Anchored in Sherry Arnstein's "Ladder of Citizen Participation," the platform is designed to operate at Rung 7 (Delegated Power). The World Café engine guarantees that citizens have structural authority over process design.

Rather than a rigid linear process, the assembly uses cyclical Reflection-Action loops (Nico Carpentier). Citizens can pause, review AI-clustered feedback, and adjust the deliberative rules, actively shifting epistemic power from facilitators to participants. The Expert Removal Protocol ensures that institutional knowledge informs but does not dominate citizen deliberation.

Key innovation: The PAR cycle is operationalized digitally through the World Café Engine, which manages bicommunal input, enforces cognitive equity through algorithmic design, and generates bridging proposals from citizen-generated semantic clusters — not expert assumptions.

The Intracommunal Mirror: Why It Matters

Most peacebuilding platforms focus on intercommunal dialogue. EIRINI adds a significantly under-explored dimension: intracommunal discourse — how each community talks about the other amongst itself. People communicate differently depending on their audience; the platform captures these differences as data.

Key hypothesis: Social pressure within each community — not fundamental incompatibility between them — is the primary obstacle to a settlement gaining public acceptance. If correct, the most impactful intervention is not more dialogue between the two sides, but greater honesty within each side about what they're actually willing to accept.

Data Ethics & Anonymity

All responses are anonymous. Community identification is voluntary. No individual response is ever displayed — only aggregate patterns and clusters. The AI processes text for semantic positioning and sentiment analysis; it does not store or display original text in identifiable form. Users can update or delete their responses at any time.

The platform does not sell data, does not use data for advertising, and does not share individual-level data with any party including CPDC, the UN, or either government. No third-party analytics. No tracking cookies.

Relationship to the Consultative Body

In July 2025, the leaders agreed to establish a consultative body for civil society engagement. Terms of reference remain under discussion. EIRINI is designed as the digital infrastructure for this body — ensuring that representatives are informed by continuous, large-scale citizen input rather than relying solely on their own networks. The platform does not replace the formal body; it makes it more representative, more transparent, and more legitimate.
